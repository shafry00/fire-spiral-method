## PRAKTEKKAN — 3 Sessions (Fast-Forward)

### Session 1: Building Analogies

**Goal:** Create analogies for "Claude AI for Marketing" that both Gen Z and Boomers understand.

**Initial approach:** Single analogy — "It's like having Jarvis, but for making money."

**Gap Log:**

| # | Gap |
|---|-----|
| 1 | **Thought:** One perfect analogy covers everyone. **Reality:** Gen Z instantly connected (they know Iron Man). Boomers: "What's Jarvis?" — the analogy became a new obstacle. **Insight:** Cross-generational teaching requires **layered analogies**, not one perfect one. Three layers: anchor (specific to 1 generation), bridge (universal, all generations), hook (life experience relatable). |
| 2 | **Thought:** Simple = replace technical words. "LLM" → "smart machine." Done. **Reality:** The problem isn't vocabulary — it's **unacknowledged assumptions.** Saying "it's like ChatGPT" assumes the listener has used ChatGPT. Boomer who's never opened it is now doubly confused. **Insight:** Simplification is not synonym replacement. It's **assumption removal.** Practical technique: explain to a blind person who has never held a phone. If they understand, everyone understands. |

### Session 2: Designing the 5-Minute Task

**Goal:** Create a 5-minute Claude task that anyone can complete independently.

**Gap Log:**

| # | Gap |
|---|-----|
| 3 | **Thought:** The task should be comprehensive — make them "feel the result." **Reality:** The most effective task was **1 step, 1 result, 1 click.** "Generate 3 Instagram captions for [product]." Not a 3-month marketing strategy. **Insight:** Session 1's goal is not "they understand Claude." It's **"they think they can do this."** Confidence before comprehension. One small success → they return next week. One big frustration → they don't. |

### Session 3: Tester Validation

**Goal:** Test the analogies + task with 1 Gen Z and 1 Boomer.

**Results:**

| | Gen Z | Boomer |
|---|-------|--------|
| **Initial reaction** | Instant connection to Jarvis analogy | "Jarvis? What's that?" |
| **Bridge needed** | None — anchor worked | "It's like texting your best employee, but digital and never sleeps" |
| **Task completion** | Under 2 minutes, even asked "can I also ask Claude to make variations?" | After bridge: "Wait, that's it? That's so easy!" |
| **Confidence** | Immediate, asked for more | Shift from skeptical to curious |

## Key Insight

This case study produced **four framework-level additions:**

### 1. Layered Analogies

Cross-generational teaching requires three layers:
- **Anchor** — specific to one generation (e.g., "Jarvis" → Gen Z/Millennials)
- **Bridge** — universal, all generations ("a personal assistant that never sleeps")
- **Hook** — life experience relatable ("ever had an employee who's fast and never complains? This is that, but digital.")

### 2. Simplification = Assumption Removal

"Baby talk" is not about vocabulary. It's about **identifying and removing hidden assumptions.** The technique: explain to a blind person who has never held a phone. If they understand, everyone understands.

### 3. Confidence-First Teaching

Session 1 goal: "I can do this" — not "I understand this." One small success builds the trust needed for deeper learning in sessions 2-4. 1 step, 1 result — not 1 comprehensive project.

### 4. Witness Rule

For domains without an objective verifier: 1 independent observer + 3 testers + end-to-end documentation = sufficient validation. You don't need 100% success rate. You need 1 fully documented success case. The framework doesn't need absolute objectivity — it needs **interpretive honesty.**
